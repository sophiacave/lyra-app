Understanding Reward Signals

The reward signal is the sole mechanism through which an agent learns about the quality of its actions. At each timestep, the agent occupies a state, takes an action, transitions to a new state, and receives a scalar reward. This simple loop — s, a, r, s' — is the foundation of all reinforcement learning. The agent has no teacher providing correct answers; it must discover good behavior purely through trial and reward.

In this simulation, you play the role of the environment's reward function. When the agent moves toward the goal (bottom-right corner), a positive reward reinforces that action. When it moves away or into walls, a negative reward discourages it. Over time, you'll see the Q-table heatmap evolve: brighter cells represent state-action pairs the agent has learned to value highly.

This illustrates the credit assignment problem — one of the deepest challenges in RL. When an agent receives a reward, which of its past actions deserves credit? If the agent took 10 steps to reach the goal, all 10 actions contributed, but some more than others. The temporal structure of rewards makes learning far harder than in supervised settings where feedback is immediate and direct.

Temporal difference (TD) learning addresses this by updating value estimates incrementally. In plain English: after each action, the agent asks "Was this better or worse than I expected?" and nudges its estimate accordingly. The formula below captures this idea:

Q(s,a) ← Q(s,a) + α[r + γ max Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)]

Reading this left to right: take the old estimate Q(s,a), then adjust it by a small step (α = learning rate) toward a better estimate — the actual reward r we just received, plus the discounted (γ) value of the best next action, minus what we previously expected. Watch the heatmap as you provide rewards: you're witnessing TD learning propagate value information backward through the state space, step by step.
