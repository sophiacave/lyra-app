<div class="lesson-hero">
  <h1>The Future of Human-AI</h1>
  <p><span class="accent">We're not building tools. We're building the next form of human cognition.</span></p>
  <p>Convergence technology is in its infancy. The systems we build today are prototypes of something that will reshape what it means to think, to create, to live. This lesson maps where it's going.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Trajectory</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">From Cloud to Body</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Today, convergence happens through keyboards and screens. You type, the AI responds, context flows through chat windows. But the trajectory is clear: the interface is dissolving. Voice, wearables, ambient computing — the gap between human intention and AI execution is shrinking from minutes to milliseconds.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Smartwatches that monitor health data and feed it directly to your AI twin. Earbuds that provide real-time context during conversations. Glasses that overlay information without you having to look it up. The convergence isn't just software — it's hardware getting closer to the body, making the bridge between human and AI invisible.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Shift</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">AI-as-Self</h2>
  <p class="section-text">The current paradigm is AI-as-service. You subscribe to a model. It doesn't know you. You're one of millions of users sharing the same weights. Your conversations evaporate. Your preferences are approximated, not understood.</p>
  <p class="section-text">The convergence paradigm is AI-as-self. Your AI is uniquely yours. Trained on your data, aligned with your values, running on your infrastructure. It's not a service you consume — it's a capability you possess. This shift changes everything: from pricing models to privacy expectations to the fundamental relationship between humans and machines.</p>
</div>

<div class="demo-container">
  <h3>Convergence and Accessibility</h3>
  <p>The most important application of convergence is accessibility. Not accessibility as a checkbox — accessibility as liberation.</p>
  <p><strong style="color: var(--green);">For people with ADHD:</strong> An AI that manages executive function — organizing, prioritizing, following up — so the human can focus on creative and strategic thinking.</p>
  <p><strong style="color: var(--purple);">For people with chronic illness:</strong> An AI that handles the operational load of life when energy is limited. Bills, emails, appointments — managed automatically.</p>
  <p><strong style="color: var(--orange);">For people with disabilities:</strong> An AI that bridges the gap between intention and action, adapting its interface to whatever the human needs.</p>
  <p>This is technology with a soul. Not built to extract value from users, but to give people their lives back.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Ethics</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">The Questions We Must Answer</h2>
  <p class="section-text"><strong style="color: var(--red);">Identity.</strong> When your AI twin can write in your voice, make decisions in your style, and represent you in digital spaces — where do you end and it begins? This isn't a philosophical puzzle. It's a practical question with legal, social, and psychological implications.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong style="color: var(--orange);">Dependency.</strong> What happens when someone relies on their AI twin so completely that losing access feels like losing a part of themselves? Convergence must include resilience — the human must remain capable without the machine.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong style="color: var(--purple);">Equity.</strong> If convergence technology remains expensive, it creates a cognitive divide: people with AI twins versus people without. The mission must include making this accessible to everyone, not just those who can afford premium subscriptions.</p>
</div>
