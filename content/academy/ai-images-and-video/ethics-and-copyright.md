<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Why This Matters</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Creating with AI is powerful. Understanding the rules makes you professional.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">AI-generated visuals raise genuine questions about ownership, creativity, and responsibility. These are not abstract philosophical debates — they affect what you can legally do with the images you create, how you should credit your work, and how to be a good citizen in the creative community. Taking fifteen minutes to understand this now saves you real problems later.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Copyright Basics</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">The law is still catching up, but here is what we know.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">As of 2026, copyright law around AI-generated content is evolving rapidly. The general landscape:</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>In the United States:</strong> The Copyright Office has indicated that purely AI-generated images (with no significant human creative input) are generally not copyrightable. However, if you substantially modify, arrange, or creatively direct the output, your human contribution may be protectable. The more creative control you exercise, the stronger your claim.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>In the EU:</strong> Similar principles apply. The emphasis is on "originality" stemming from human creative choices.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>Practically speaking:</strong> If you use AI as a tool in a larger creative process — generating an image, editing it, combining it with other elements, adding your own design work — the final product has a much stronger copyright position than a raw, unedited AI generation.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Commercial Use Rights</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Can you sell or use AI images in your business? Usually yes, but check the terms.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Each platform has its own terms of service regarding commercial use:</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>DALL-E / ChatGPT:</strong> OpenAI grants you ownership of the images you generate, including commercial use rights. This applies to both free and paid tiers.<br>
  <strong>Midjourney:</strong> Paid subscribers own their generations and can use them commercially. Free tier users have more limited rights — check the current terms.<br>
  <strong>Stable Diffusion:</strong> Open source, so the model itself is free to use commercially. But specific fine-tuned models may have their own licenses.<br>
  <strong>Adobe Firefly:</strong> Designed specifically for commercial safety. Trained on licensed content. Adobe provides an IP indemnity for enterprise customers.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Always read the terms of service for your specific tool and plan. These terms can change, and what was true six months ago may not be true today.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Ethics Conversation</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">AI art exists in relationship with human artists. Respect that relationship.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">AI image models were trained on human-created art and photography. This raises valid concerns from the creative community:</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>Training data:</strong> Many artists feel their work was used without consent to train these models. This is a legitimate concern, and it is driving changes in how new models are trained (Adobe Firefly's licensed-data approach, for example).</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>Impact on creative professionals:</strong> AI tools can create in seconds what used to take hours. This affects livelihoods. Being aware of this impact — and continuing to value and support human artists — is part of being a responsible AI user.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>Deepfakes and misuse:</strong> AI can generate realistic images of real people without their consent. This is harmful and, in many jurisdictions, illegal. Never generate images of real people without their permission. Never create misleading or deceptive content.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Best Practices</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">A simple code of conduct for responsible AI visual creation.</h2>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>Be transparent:</strong> When you use AI-generated visuals, be honest about it when asked. Many creators add "Created with AI" to their social posts or portfolio pieces. This builds trust.<br>
  <strong>Add your own creativity:</strong> Use AI as a starting point, not the whole process. Edit, combine, design on top of, and curate your AI outputs. Your creative choices are what make the work yours.<br>
  <strong>Support human artists:</strong> Use AI to supplement, not replace, your engagement with the creative community. Hire illustrators for important projects. Credit inspiration.<br>
  <strong>Stay informed:</strong> Copyright law and platform terms change. Check in periodically to make sure your practices align with current rules.<br>
  <strong>Never deceive:</strong> Do not present AI-generated images as real photographs. Do not create images of real people without consent. Do not use AI visuals to mislead or manipulate.</p>
</div>

<div class="try-it-box" style="border: 2px solid var(--green); border-radius: 8px; padding: 1.5rem; margin: 2rem 0; background: var(--bg);">
  <h3 style="color: var(--green);">Try it now</h3>
  <p>Go to the terms of service page for the AI image tool you use most. Find the section on ownership and commercial use. Read it carefully and save the key points somewhere you can reference them. This takes five minutes and gives you confidence that you are using the tool within its rules. If you use multiple tools, do this for each one.</p>
