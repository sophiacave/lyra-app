<div class="lesson-hero">
  <h1>Finding AI Product Ideas</h1>
  <p><span class="accent">The best AI products aren't invented. They're discovered.</span></p>
  <p>You find them by watching people struggle with tasks that are tedious, expensive, or require expertise they don't have.</p>
</div>

<div class="learn-card">
  <h3>What you'll learn</h3>
  <ul>
    <li>Where to look for high-value AI product opportunities</li>
    <li>The "spreadsheet test" for identifying automation candidates</li>
    <li>How to evaluate whether AI actually improves the solution</li>
    <li>Red flags that signal a bad AI product idea</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Method</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">The Spreadsheet Test</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Find someone using a spreadsheet for something a spreadsheet was never meant to do. That's your opportunity. People using Excel to manage content calendars, track customer sentiment, categorize support tickets — these are all workflows begging for AI.</p>
  <p class="section-text">The spreadsheet is a signal that the problem is real, the user already cares enough to build a janky solution, and no existing tool serves them well enough. That's the sweet spot.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Strategy</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Five Hunting Grounds for AI Ideas</h2>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>1. Expert bottlenecks.</strong> Where do companies pay expensive humans to do repetitive cognitive work? Legal document review. Medical image screening. Code review. Wherever expertise creates a bottleneck, AI can widen the pipe.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>2. Translation gaps.</strong> Anywhere information exists in one form but is needed in another. Audio to text. Images to descriptions. Data to narratives. Jargon to plain language.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>3. Decision fatigue.</strong> People making hundreds of small decisions daily — what to post, what to prioritize, what to respond to. AI as a decision-support layer, not a decision-maker.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>4. Personalization deserts.</strong> Services that treat every user identically when they shouldn't. Education, health recommendations, financial advice — anywhere one-size-fits-all is the norm because personalization was too expensive.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>5. Your own pain.</strong> The most honest ideas come from problems you personally face. You understand the nuance, the frustration, the workarounds. Build for yourself first.</p>
</div>

<div class="demo-container">
  <h3>Idea Evaluation Scorecard</h3>
  <p><strong style="color: var(--green);">Strong signal:</strong> "I currently pay someone $50/hr to do this" or "I spend 3 hours a week on this"</p>
  <p><strong style="color: var(--orange);">Medium signal:</strong> "This would be nice to have" or "I sometimes struggle with this"</p>
  <p><strong style="color: var(--red);">Weak signal:</strong> "AI could theoretically do this" or "This would be cool"</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Warning</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Red Flags — Ideas to Avoid</h2>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>The "AI for AI's sake" trap.</strong> If you can't explain the value without mentioning AI, the idea is technology-driven, not problem-driven. Nobody cares about your model. They care about their problem disappearing.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>The accuracy trap.</strong> If your product requires 99.9% accuracy to be useful (medical diagnosis, legal compliance, financial transactions), you're building on a foundation that current AI can't guarantee. Look for domains where 85% accuracy is still 10x better than the status quo.</p>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>The "just a prompt" trap.</strong> If your entire idea is a system prompt wrapped in a UI, a competitor can clone you in an afternoon. Ideas need a data moat, a workflow advantage, or a network effect.</p>
</div>

<div class="try-it-box">
  <h3>Try It Yourself</h3>
  <p>List three tasks you did this week that were tedious, repetitive, or required translating information from one format to another. For each one, ask:</p>
  <div class="prompt-box"><code>1. Could AI do 80% of this work?
2. Would I pay $20/month to never do it again?
3. Do other people have this same problem?

If all three are yes — you have a product idea worth testing.</code></div>
