<div class="lesson-hero">
  <h1>Building the <span class="accent">Business Case</span></h1>
  <p class="section-text">Every AI initiative lives or dies on the business case. Not the technology demo. Not the vendor pitch. The clear, compelling story of why this investment will return more value than the alternatives. This lesson teaches you to build that story so well that budget approval becomes inevitable.</p>
</div>

<div class="learn-card">
  <h3>What you will learn</h3>

    <li>The stakeholder mapping framework that turns skeptics into sponsors</li>
    <li>Pilot design principles that maximize learning and minimize risk</li>
    <li>How to structure a business case document that executives actually read</li>
  </ul>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">ROI Framework</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">The Real Math Behind AI Investment</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Vendor ROI projections are fiction. They assume perfect implementation, instant adoption, and zero organizational friction. Your business case needs honest numbers. Start with the cost of the problem you are solving — not the value of the solution you are buying.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Quantify the current cost: labor hours, error rates, customer churn, missed opportunities, compliance risk. Then estimate what a 20% improvement would save. Not 80%. Not 50%. Twenty percent. If the investment pays off at 20% improvement, you have a robust case. Everything above that is upside.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Include total cost of ownership: licensing, infrastructure, integration, training, ongoing maintenance, and the opportunity cost of the team building it. AI projects that look cheap on day one get expensive by month six if you have not accounted for the full picture.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Stakeholder Mapping</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Turn Skeptics Into Sponsors</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Every AI business case has four audiences: the executive sponsor who controls budget, the operational leader whose team will use it, the IT leader who must integrate it, and the finance leader who must justify it. Each needs a different story told in their language.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Map each stakeholder's concerns before you present. The CEO wants competitive advantage. The COO wants efficiency. The CTO wants architectural fit. The CFO wants payback period. One business case, four narratives. This is not manipulation — it is communication.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Pilot Design</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Small Bets, Big Lessons</h2>
  <p class="section-text">The best AI pilots share three characteristics: a clearly defined problem, a measurable success metric, and a 90-day timeline. Anything longer and you lose momentum. Anything vaguer and you cannot prove value.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Choose a pilot that is painful enough to matter but contained enough to manage. Customer service ticket classification, invoice processing, demand forecasting for a single product line. Win small, then scale with evidence.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Like One Perspective</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Honesty Is Strategy</h2>
  <p class="section-text">The most powerful thing you can do in an AI business case is be honest about what you do not know. Executives are tired of hype. A business case that says "here is what we expect, here is what we are uncertain about, and here is how we will learn" earns more trust than one promising the moon. Credibility is your greatest asset.</p>
</div>
