  <h1>Vendor <span class="accent">Evaluation</span></h1>
  <p class="sub">The AI vendor landscape is a minefield of bold claims, overlapping capabilities, and pricing models designed to confuse. This lesson gives you a framework to cut through the noise.</p>
</div>

<div class="content">

<div class="card">
<h2>The AI Vendor Gold Rush</h2>
<p>There are now over <strong style="color:#e5e5e5">4,000 AI startups</strong> competing for enterprise budgets, plus every major cloud provider and legacy software company rebranding features as "AI-powered." The result is a landscape where it is nearly impossible to distinguish genuine capability from marketing veneer without a structured evaluation process.</p>

<p>The cost of choosing wrong is not just wasted budget. It is 6-12 months of integration work that has to be unwound, organizational trust in AI that erodes, and a team that becomes cynical about the next AI initiative. Getting vendor selection right is worth investing significant time in — the upfront evaluation cost is a rounding error compared to the cost of a bad choice.</p>

<p>This lesson gives you the frameworks, red flags, and contract strategies to make decisions you will not regret in 18 months.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
<h2>Build vs. Buy vs. Partner: The First Decision</h2>
<p>Before evaluating vendors, ask whether you should be talking to vendors at all. The build/buy/partner decision should be made deliberately, not defaulted into.</p>

<div style="display:grid;gap:.75rem;margin-top:1rem">
<div style="padding:1.25rem;border-radius:10px;background:rgba(139,92,246,.04);border:1px solid rgba(139,92,246,.1)">
<div style="display:flex;justify-content:space-between;align-items:center;margin-bottom:.5rem">
<strong style="color:#8b5cf6;font-size:.9rem">Build In-House</strong>
<span style="background:rgba(139,92,246,.12);color:#8b5cf6;padding:.15rem .6rem;border-radius:6px;font-size:.72rem;font-weight:600">WHEN AI = YOUR PRODUCT</span>
</div>
<p style="font-size:.85rem;color:#a1a1aa;margin:0;line-height:1.7">Choose when AI is your core competitive advantage and you need full control over the model, the data, and the roadmap. Requires significant in-house talent (ML engineers, data engineers, MLOps) and infrastructure investment. The ego of custom development is seductive but expensive — unless AI <em>is</em> your product, strongly reconsider.</p>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.3rem 0 0"><strong>Best for:</strong> AI-native products, proprietary algorithms that are your moat, highly regulated industries where you must control every aspect of the model.</p>
</div>
<div style="padding:1.25rem;border-radius:10px;background:rgba(52,211,153,.04);border:1px solid rgba(52,211,153,.1)">
<div style="display:flex;justify-content:space-between;align-items:center;margin-bottom:.5rem">
<strong style="color:#34d399;font-size:.9rem">Buy (Commercial Solution)</strong>
<span style="background:rgba(52,211,153,.12);color:#34d399;padding:.15rem .6rem;border-radius:6px;font-size:.72rem;font-weight:600">MOST COMMON</span>
</div>
<p style="font-size:.85rem;color:#a1a1aa;margin:0;line-height:1.7">Choose when the problem is well-understood and commercial solutions are mature. Customer support AI, document processing, basic analytics, code assistance, content generation. Faster time to value, lower upfront cost, vendor handles maintenance and updates. The trade-off: less customization and dependency on vendor roadmap.</p>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.3rem 0 0"><strong>Best for:</strong> First 2-3 AI use cases, well-defined problems, organizations building AI muscle before bringing capabilities in-house.</p>
</div>
<div style="padding:1.25rem;border-radius:10px;background:rgba(251,146,60,.04);border:1px solid rgba(251,146,60,.1)">
<div style="display:flex;justify-content:space-between;align-items:center;margin-bottom:.5rem">
<strong style="color:#fb923c;font-size:.9rem">Partner (Consulting/Boutique)</strong>
<span style="background:rgba(251,146,60,.12);color:#fb923c;padding:.15rem .6rem;border-radius:6px;font-size:.72rem;font-weight:600">ACCELERATOR</span>
</div>
<p style="font-size:.85rem;color:#a1a1aa;margin:0;line-height:1.7">Choose when you need domain-specific AI but lack internal capability. Consulting firms and boutique AI shops can accelerate your timeline by 6-12 months. The critical factor: ensure knowledge transfer is contractually required. A partner that builds something only they can maintain has created a dependency, not a partnership.</p>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.3rem 0 0"><strong>Best for:</strong> Complex domain-specific problems, temporary capability gaps, accelerating time-to-market while building internal team.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div style="background:rgba(56,189,248,.06);border:1px solid rgba(56,189,248,.12);border-radius:12px;padding:1rem;margin-top:1rem;font-size:.82rem;color:#a1a1aa;line-height:1.7">
<strong style="color:#e5e5e5">The pragmatic path:</strong> Most organizations over-index on building. Unless AI is your product, strongly consider buying or partnering for your first 2-3 use cases. Build institutional knowledge. Learn what works in your organization. <em>Then</em> bring capabilities in-house strategically for use cases where custom AI becomes a genuine competitive advantage.
</div>
</div>

<div class="card">
<h2>The Six-Dimension Evaluation Framework</h2>
<p>Feature checklists are how vendors want you to evaluate them — because they control which features are on the list. Instead, evaluate across six dimensions that matter for long-term success:</p>

<div style="overflow-x:auto;margin-top:1rem">
<table style="width:100%;border-collapse:collapse;font-size:.82rem">
<thead>
<tr style="border-bottom:1px solid rgba(255,255,255,.1)">
<th style="text-align:left;padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">Dimension</th>
<th style="text-align:left;padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">What to Evaluate</th>
<th style="text-align:left;padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">Weight</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody style="color:#a1a1aa">
<tr style="border-bottom:1px solid rgba(255,255,255,.05)">
<td style="padding:.75rem;font-weight:600;color:#34d399">Capability Fit</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem">Does it solve your actual problem — not a related problem, your specific problem? Run a POC with your data. Vendor demos use curated scenarios.</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">25%</td>
</tr>
<tr style="border-bottom:1px solid rgba(255,255,255,.05)">
<td style="padding:.75rem;font-weight:600;color:#8b5cf6">Integration Complexity</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem">How hard is it to connect to your existing systems? API quality, authentication, data format compatibility, real-time vs. batch. Ask for integration architecture diagrams, not marketing slides.</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">20%</td>
</tr>
<tr style="border-bottom:1px solid rgba(255,255,255,.05)">
<td style="padding:.75rem;font-weight:600;color:#fb923c">Total Cost</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem">Licensing + implementation + training + ongoing maintenance + scaling costs. Get pricing for 1x, 5x, and 10x your current volume. Many vendors price attractively at pilot scale and become prohibitive at production scale.</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">20%</td>
</tr>
<tr style="border-bottom:1px solid rgba(255,255,255,.05)">
<td style="padding:.75rem;font-weight:600;color:#f472b6">Vendor Viability</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem">Will they exist in 3 years? Revenue, funding, customer count, team size, product roadmap maturity. In the current AI bubble, many startups will not survive the consolidation. Check Crunchbase, ask for customer retention numbers.</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">15%</td>
</tr>
<tr style="border-bottom:1px solid rgba(255,255,255,.05)">
<td style="padding:.75rem;font-weight:600;color:#38bdf8">Data Handling</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem">Where does your data go? Who can access it? Is it used to train the vendor's models? Is it encrypted at rest and in transit? Can you delete it? GDPR/CCPA compliance. This dimension alone has killed deals and cost companies millions in regulatory fines.</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">10%</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td style="padding:.75rem;font-weight:600;color:#eab308">Exit Strategy</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem">How painful and expensive is it to leave? Proprietary data formats, lock-in mechanisms, data export capabilities, contract termination terms. The best vendors make it easy to leave because they are confident you will not want to.</td>
<td style="padding:.75rem;color:#e5e5e5;font-weight:600">10%</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
</div>

<p style="margin-top:1rem;font-size:.85rem;color:#a1a1aa;line-height:1.7"><strong style="color:#e5e5e5">Critical rule:</strong> Run a proof of concept with your actual data on your actual problem. Vendor demos use curated data on ideal scenarios. You need to see performance on your messy, real-world data. Any vendor unwilling to do a POC on your data is not confident in their product.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
<h2>Red Flags: When to Walk Away</h2>
<p>Not every red flag means "do not buy." But each one should trigger deeper investigation. If you see three or more, walk away.</p>

<div style="display:grid;gap:.75rem;margin-top:1rem">
<div style="display:flex;gap:.75rem;padding:.75rem 1rem;background:rgba(239,68,68,.04);border-radius:10px;border:1px solid rgba(239,68,68,.08)">
<div style="font-size:1.1rem;min-width:1.5rem">🚩</div>
<div>
<strong style="color:#ef4444;font-size:.85rem">Accuracy claims without methodology</strong>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.2rem 0 0;line-height:1.6">"98% accuracy" means nothing without knowing: what dataset, what metric, what conditions. If they cannot share the evaluation methodology, the number is marketing, not science.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="display:flex;gap:.75rem;padding:.75rem 1rem;background:rgba(239,68,68,.04);border-radius:10px;border:1px solid rgba(239,68,68,.08)">
<div style="font-size:1.1rem;min-width:1.5rem">🚩</div>
<div>
<strong style="color:#ef4444;font-size:.85rem">Annual commitment required before a pilot</strong>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.2rem 0 0;line-height:1.6">A vendor that wants your money before proving value is selling vaporware or knows their product underperforms in real conditions. Demand a paid pilot (30-90 days) before any annual commitment.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="display:flex;gap:.75rem;padding:.75rem 1rem;background:rgba(239,68,68,.04);border-radius:10px;border:1px solid rgba(239,68,68,.08)">
<div style="font-size:1.1rem;min-width:1.5rem">🚩</div>
<div>
<strong style="color:#ef4444;font-size:.85rem">Proprietary data formats with no export</strong>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.2rem 0 0;line-height:1.6">If your data goes into a format only their system can read, you are trapped. Any modern vendor should export to standard formats (JSON, CSV, Parquet) with full data portability.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="display:flex;gap:.75rem;padding:.75rem 1rem;background:rgba(239,68,68,.04);border-radius:10px;border:1px solid rgba(239,68,68,.08)">
<div style="font-size:1.1rem;min-width:1.5rem">🚩</div>
<div>
<strong style="color:#ef4444;font-size:.85rem">Cannot name referenceable enterprise customers</strong>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.2rem 0 0;line-height:1.6">If a vendor claims enterprise traction but cannot provide 3 customers you can actually call and ask about their experience, the traction is not real. Always call the references — and ask about what went wrong, not just what went right.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="display:flex;gap:.75rem;padding:.75rem 1rem;background:rgba(239,68,68,.04);border-radius:10px;border:1px solid rgba(239,68,68,.08)">
<div style="font-size:1.1rem;min-width:1.5rem">🚩</div>
<div>
<strong style="color:#ef4444;font-size:.85rem">Sales team cannot explain how the AI works</strong>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.2rem 0 0;line-height:1.6">You do not need them to explain backpropagation. But they should be able to explain in plain language: what data it uses, how it makes decisions, what it gets wrong, and how it improves over time. If the sales team hides behind "proprietary technology," ask for a technical deep-dive with their engineering team.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="display:flex;gap:.75rem;padding:.75rem 1rem;background:rgba(239,68,68,.04);border-radius:10px;border:1px solid rgba(239,68,68,.08)">
<div style="font-size:1.1rem;min-width:1.5rem">🚩</div>
<div>
<strong style="color:#ef4444;font-size:.85rem">Your data is used to train their models (without clear opt-out)</strong>
<p style="font-size:.78rem;color:#71717a;margin:.2rem 0 0;line-height:1.6">If your proprietary business data improves their product for your competitors, you are subsidizing their R&D. Enterprise contracts must include clear data usage terms, opt-out from model training, and data deletion rights.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>

<div class="card">
<h2>Contract and Pilot Structure</h2>
<p>How you structure the deal determines how much leverage you retain. Here are the contract elements that protect your organization:</p>

<div style="display:grid;gap:.75rem;margin-top:1rem">
<div style="padding:1rem 1.25rem;border-radius:10px;background:rgba(52,211,153,.04);border:1px solid rgba(52,211,153,.1)">
<strong style="color:#34d399;font-size:.88rem">Pilot First, Always</strong>
<p style="font-size:.82rem;color:#a1a1aa;margin:.4rem 0 0;line-height:1.7">30-90 day paid pilot with your data, your use case, your success criteria. The pilot cost should be credited toward the annual contract if you proceed. If the vendor will not agree to this, they are not confident in their product.</p>
</div>
<div style="padding:1rem 1.25rem;border-radius:10px;background:rgba(139,92,246,.04);border:1px solid rgba(139,92,246,.1)">
<strong style="color:#8b5cf6;font-size:.88rem">Performance SLAs</strong>
<p style="font-size:.82rem;color:#a1a1aa;margin:.4rem 0 0;line-height:1.7">Define measurable performance thresholds (accuracy, latency, uptime) with financial penalties if missed. Vague SLAs ("commercially reasonable efforts") are worthless. Get specific numbers.</p>
</div>
<div style="padding:1rem 1.25rem;border-radius:10px;background:rgba(251,146,60,.04);border:1px solid rgba(251,146,60,.1)">
<strong style="color:#fb923c;font-size:.88rem">Data Rights and Portability</strong>
<p style="font-size:.82rem;color:#a1a1aa;margin:.4rem 0 0;line-height:1.7">Your data remains yours. Full export in standard formats at any time. No use of your data for training without explicit opt-in. Data deletion within 30 days of contract termination. These are non-negotiable.</p>
</div>
<div style="padding:1rem 1.25rem;border-radius:10px;background:rgba(56,189,248,.04);border:1px solid rgba(56,189,248,.1)">
<strong style="color:#38bdf8;font-size:.88rem">Exit Clause</strong>
<p style="font-size:.82rem;color:#a1a1aa;margin:.4rem 0 0;line-height:1.7">90-day termination notice maximum. No penalties for termination after the initial term. Data export assistance included. The best vendors make it easy to leave because they are confident you will stay.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>

<div class="card">
<h2>Your Vendor Is Your Partner</h2>
<p>The best vendor relationships are partnerships, not transactions. Find vendors who invest in your success, not just your contract. Who offer training, not just software. Who share their product roadmap, not just release notes. Who assign a dedicated customer success manager, not just a sales rep who disappears after the deal closes.</p>

<p>Technology changes fast — a vendor relationship is a bet on a team, not just a product. The product will evolve. The question is whether the team behind it has the talent, the funding, and the commitment to evolve it in a direction that serves your needs.</p>

<p style="font-size:.85rem;color:#a1a1aa;line-height:1.7">Ask yourself: if this vendor's product disappeared tomorrow, would they help you migrate — or would they make it as painful as possible? The answer tells you whether you have a partner or a landlord.</p>
</div>
