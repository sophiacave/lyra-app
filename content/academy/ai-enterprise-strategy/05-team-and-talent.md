  <h1>Team and <span class="accent">Talent</span></h1>
  <p class="section-text">You do not need a hundred data scientists. You need the right people in the right roles with the right support. This lesson breaks down how to build, hire, and organize an AI team that actually delivers — even if you are starting from zero.</p>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Seven Roles</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Build the Right Team, Not the Biggest Team</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Every AI team needs these capabilities: <strong>AI/ML engineer</strong> (builds models), <strong>data engineer</strong> (builds pipelines), <strong>product manager</strong> (translates business to technical), <strong>domain expert</strong> (knows the problem deeply), <strong>MLOps engineer</strong> (deploys and monitors), <strong>ethicist or governance lead</strong> (ensures responsible use), and <strong>executive sponsor</strong> (protects budget and removes blockers).</p>
  <p class="section-text">You do not need seven hires. One person can cover multiple roles early on. But every capability must exist. The most common missing role is the product manager — someone who can stand between the business and the data scientists and translate fluently in both directions. Hire that person first.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Upskilling Over Hiring</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Your Best AI Talent Already Works for You</h2>
  <p class="section-text">The market for AI talent is brutal and expensive. But your existing employees have something no external hire can bring: deep knowledge of your business, your customers, and your data. A domain expert who learns prompt engineering delivers more value in month one than a brilliant data scientist who spends six months learning your industry.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Create structured learning paths. Dedicate 10% of work time to AI skill development. Partner junior developers with senior AI practitioners. Use AI tools themselves to accelerate learning — the best way to understand AI is to use it daily on real problems.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Organizational Models</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Where Does the AI Team Sit?</h2>
  <p class="section-text"><strong>Centralized:</strong> One AI team serves the whole organization. Good for consistency and governance. Bad for responsiveness and business alignment. <strong>Embedded:</strong> AI practitioners sit within business units. Good for relevance. Bad for knowledge sharing and standards. <strong>Hub-and-spoke:</strong> A central team sets standards and builds platforms while embedded practitioners apply them in business units. This is where most mature organizations land.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Start centralized to build capability. Move to hub-and-spoke as you scale. The transition typically happens around your third or fourth production AI system.</p>
</div>

<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Like One Perspective</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">People Are the Strategy</h2>
  <p class="section-text">AI does not replace people. It amplifies them. The organizations that get this right treat AI as a tool that makes every employee more capable, not a replacement that makes employees nervous. Build your team with that philosophy and you will attract talent that your competitors cannot.</p>
