<!-- SECTION 1: COPYRIGHT -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Legal Landscape</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Copyright and AI: what we know, what we don't.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Let's be honest: the legal landscape around AI-generated content is still being written. Courts are making decisions right now that will shape this for decades. But here's what we know today -- and what you need to know as a working creative.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>What's Generally Accepted</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>Purely AI-generated content (no human creative input) is generally <strong>not copyrightable</strong> in the US</li>
        <li>Content where AI assisted but a human made substantial creative decisions <strong>likely is</strong> copyrightable</li>
        <li>Your prompts alone probably don't qualify as sufficient creative input</li>
        <li>Editing, curating, and arranging AI output adds human authorship</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>What's Still Unclear</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>Exactly how much human input makes something copyrightable</li>
        <li>Whether AI training on copyrighted works is fair use</li>
        <li>How different countries will regulate AI content</li>
        <li>Whether AI-generated images infringe on artists' styles</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>The practical takeaway:</strong> The more human creative involvement in the final work, the stronger your copyright claim. Use AI as a tool in your process, not as the entire process. Edit substantially. Make real creative decisions. Add things only you could add. This isn't just good ethics -- it's good legal strategy.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Check Your Platform</div>
    <p>Every AI tool has different terms of service regarding ownership of outputs. Some give you full commercial rights. Some don't. Some claim the right to use your outputs to train future models. <strong>Read the terms for every tool you use professionally.</strong> Five minutes of reading now saves a legal headache later.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 2: HOMOGENIZATION -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Real Risk</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">When AI helps vs. when it homogenizes.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Here's the uncomfortable truth that nobody selling AI tools wants to talk about: <strong>AI has a default voice. And if you're not careful, your work will start sounding like everyone else's.</strong></p>
  <p class="section-text">AI is trained on the average of everything. It gravitates toward the median. The most common structures, the most popular phrases, the safest choices. That's the opposite of what makes creative work valuable.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Signs AI Is Helping You</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>You're producing more work without feeling drained</li>
        <li>You're exploring ideas you wouldn't have thought of alone</li>
        <li>Your editing eye is getting sharper, not lazier</li>
        <li>People still say your work sounds like YOU</li>
        <li>You're spending more time on creative decisions, less on mechanical labor</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Signs AI Is Homogenizing You</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>All your work is starting to sound the same -- and not in your voice</li>
        <li>You're accepting first drafts without meaningful editing</li>
        <li>You couldn't write this piece without AI anymore</li>
        <li>Your audience says your recent work feels "different" (and not in a good way)</li>
        <li>You're spending less time thinking and more time prompting</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>The antidote to homogenization is taste.</strong> Your taste -- the thing that makes you choose this word over that word, this color over that color, this note over that note -- is the one thing AI cannot replicate. It's your fingerprint. Sharpen it. Trust it. Never outsource it.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 3: PROTECTING YOUR VOICE -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Your Voice</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Protecting and amplifying what makes you, you.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Your creative identity isn't just what you make -- it's <strong>how</strong> you make it, <strong>why</strong> you make it, and the perspective only you can bring. AI can mimic style. It cannot replicate lived experience, genuine opinion, or authentic vulnerability.</p>

  <p class="section-text">Here are concrete strategies for keeping your voice strong in an AI-assisted workflow:</p>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Strategy 1: The 80/20 Rule</div>
    <p>Let AI handle 80% of the mechanical work -- research, first drafts, formatting, repurposing. But always do the final 20% yourself. The opening line, the key metaphor, the emotional beat, the unexpected turn. That's where your voice lives.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Strategy 2: Regular Unplugged Sessions</div>
    <p>At least once a week, create something with zero AI involvement. Write by hand. Sketch without reference images. Play without backing tracks. This keeps your creative muscles strong and prevents AI dependency. Think of it as creative cross-training.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Strategy 3: The Voice Audit</div>
    <p>Every month, pull up 5 recent pieces. Read them without knowing which parts AI helped with. Can you tell? If your AI-assisted work is indistinguishable from your fully human work in voice and quality, you're doing it right. If the AI pieces feel generic, recalibrate.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Strategy 4: Lead With Opinion</div>
    <p>AI is great at presenting balanced views. You know what it's terrible at? Having a genuine, controversial, hard-won opinion. Start every piece with what YOU actually think -- not what's safe or popular. Your take is the thing no one else can generate.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 4: DISCLOSURE -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Transparency</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">The ethics of disclosure: when and how to be honest.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Should you tell people you used AI? This question keeps creatives up at night. Here's my honest take: <strong>transparency is not just ethical -- it's a competitive advantage.</strong></p>
  <p class="section-text">Audiences increasingly respect creators who are honest about their process. Hiding AI use feels like deception. Being open about it positions you as someone who's innovative, efficient, and confident enough to be transparent.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>When Disclosure Is Essential</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>Client work where AI use isn't in the contract</li>
        <li>Journalism and factual reporting</li>
        <li>Academic writing and research</li>
        <li>Work submitted to competitions or awards</li>
        <li>Any context where people assume 100% human creation</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>When Disclosure Is Your Call</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>Personal blog posts and social content</li>
        <li>Marketing copy for your own business</li>
        <li>Creative work where AI was one tool among many</li>
        <li>Internal documents and planning</li>
        <li>Brainstorming and ideation that informed (but didn't become) the final work</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>A simple disclosure framework:</strong> "This piece was written by me with AI assistance for [research/drafting/editing]." Honest, professional, no big deal. The more normalized this becomes, the less anyone will care about whether you used AI -- and the more they'll care about whether the work is good.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Sophia's Take</div>
    <p>I use AI in almost everything I create. It's in my workflow the way spell-check was in my workflow 20 years ago -- so deeply embedded I don't think about it separately. I'm open about it because I believe the future belongs to creators who use every tool available and are honest about it. The work speaks for itself. If it's good, it's good. Period.</p>
  </div>
