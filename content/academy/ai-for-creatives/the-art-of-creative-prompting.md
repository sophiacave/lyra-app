<!-- SECTION 1: ADVANCED TECHNIQUES -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Beyond Basics</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Creative prompting is a conversation, not a command.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Most people prompt AI like they're ordering at a drive-through. "Write me a poem about the ocean." And they get exactly what that deserves -- something technically correct and completely soulless.</p>
  <p class="section-text">Creative prompting is different. You're not giving orders. You're <strong>collaborating with a mind that has read everything but experienced nothing.</strong> Your job is to bring the experience. The feeling. The "I want it to feel like driving at night with the windows down." That's what separates a prompt from a creative brief.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Sensory Anchoring</h4>
      <p>Instead of describing what you want, describe how it should <strong>feel</strong>. "Write this like it tastes like cold lemonade on a hot sidewalk." AI responds remarkably well to sensory language because it maps to rich clusters of training data.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Reference Stacking</h4>
      <p>Layer multiple references to triangulate a style. "The visual density of Wes Anderson, the emotional rawness of Phoebe Waller-Bridge, the color palette of a Rothko painting." Three references create a space no single reference can.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Constraint-Driven Creativity</h4>
      <p>Give AI creative constraints. "Write this in exactly 50 words." "Use only one-syllable words." "Every paragraph must start with a color." Constraints force AI out of its default patterns -- just like they force us out of ours.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Persona Prompting</h4>
      <p>Don't just say "write like a poet." Say "You are a poet who grew up in the Rust Belt, writes at 2am, and believes brevity is a form of respect." The more specific the persona, the more distinct the voice.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>The golden rule of creative prompting:</strong> If your prompt could apply to anyone's project, it's too generic. The best prompts contain details only YOU would think to include. Your specificity is your superpower.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 2: STYLE TRANSFER -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Your Aesthetic</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Teaching AI your style -- without losing your soul.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">This is the part most creative AI courses skip. They teach you how to get output. But they don't teach you how to get <strong>your</strong> output. Here's the method.</p>

  <p class="section-text"><strong>Step 1: Define your style in words.</strong> This is harder than it sounds. Most creatives can feel their style but can't articulate it. Spend 10 minutes writing down: What adjectives describe your work? What do people say about it? What do you never do?</p>

  <p class="section-text"><strong>Step 2: Feed AI examples of your work.</strong> Give Claude 3-5 samples of your best work and ask: "Analyze the style, tone, sentence structure, and recurring patterns in these pieces. Then describe my writing style back to me in a way I can use as an instruction for future prompts."</p>

  <p class="section-text"><strong>Step 3: Use that description as a style anchor.</strong> Paste the style description at the start of every creative prompt. Now AI isn't guessing -- it has a blueprint.</p>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Real Example: Style Anchor for a Writer</div>
    <p style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;font-style:italic">"My writing style: short sentences that hit hard. Conversational but never sloppy. I use em-dashes constantly. I lean on concrete metaphors over abstract ones. My paragraphs are rarely more than 3 sentences. I sound like I'm talking to one person over coffee, not lecturing a room. I never use the word 'utilize' or 'leverage.' I'd rather be honest than polished."</p>
    <p style="margin-top:.75rem;font-size:.85rem">Once you have this, you add it to the top of any creative prompt. Claude then generates text that actually sounds like you wrote it -- not like a chatbot wrote it.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>For visual artists:</strong> The same principle applies with image generation. Create a style reference sheet: "My aesthetic is muted earth tones, heavy grain, negative space, brutalist typography, hand-drawn texture overlays." Save it. Reuse it. Your prompts become consistent instead of random.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 3: ITERATIVE PROMPTING -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Loop</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Iterative prompting: from rough draft to refined work.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Here's something that surprises people: <strong>the first output is never the final output.</strong> Even with a perfect prompt. The magic of creative AI work happens in the iteration loop -- the back-and-forth where you shape the clay.</p>

  <p class="section-text">Think of it like this: the first prompt gets you into the neighborhood. The iterations get you to the right house.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Round 1: Generate Wide</h4>
      <p>"Give me 5 different approaches to this." Don't commit to a direction yet. Let AI show you the landscape. You're scouting, not building.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Round 2: Pick & Push</h4>
      <p>"I like approach #3 but it's too formal. Make it warmer. Keep the structure, change the tone." Now you're directing. Be specific about what works AND what doesn't.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Round 3: Refine Details</h4>
      <p>"The opening line is perfect. The third paragraph drags. Tighten it. And change 'beautiful' to something less obvious." Line-level editing. This is where your taste matters most.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Round 4: Polish & Own</h4>
      <p>Take the output into your own hands. Edit it yourself. Add your finishing touches. The AI got you 80% there fast. The last 20% is what makes it yours.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <div class="tip-label">Power Phrases for Iteration</div>
    <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
      <li>"Keep [specific thing] but change [specific thing]"</li>
      <li>"This is too [adjective]. Make it more [adjective]"</li>
      <li>"Give me a version that's the opposite of this"</li>
      <li>"What's the most unexpected way to say this?"</li>
      <li>"Rewrite this but make me feel [emotion]"</li>
      <li>"This sounds like AI wrote it. Make it sound human. Make it imperfect."</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 4: PROMPT LIBRARY -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Your Library</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Build a prompt library you'll actually use.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Every time you write a prompt that works really well, you should be saving it. Not in some elaborate system -- in a simple document you can search. A prompt library is the single highest-ROI thing a creative can build with AI.</p>

  <p class="section-text">Here's how to organize it so it doesn't become another abandoned note:</p>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>The Simple Prompt Library Format:</strong></p>
    <p style="margin-top:.75rem;font-size:.9rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8">
      <strong>Category:</strong> (e.g., Blog Intros, Social Captions, Design Briefs)<br>
      <strong>Prompt:</strong> The actual prompt text with [BRACKETS] for variable parts<br>
      <strong>When to use:</strong> One sentence on when this prompt shines<br>
      <strong>Output quality:</strong> Rate it 1-5 so you know which prompts are your best
    </p>
  </div>

  <p class="section-text">Keep your prompts in whatever tool you already use daily -- Notion, Apple Notes, a Google Doc. The best system is the one you'll actually open. Don't build a fancy database. Build a searchable list.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Starter Prompts to Save</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>Your style anchor (from Section 2)</li>
        <li>Your "brainstorm 10 ideas for [TOPIC]" prompt</li>
        <li>Your "edit this for [QUALITY]" prompt</li>
        <li>Your "repurpose this [FORMAT] into [FORMAT]" prompt</li>
        <li>Your "give me feedback on this as a [ROLE]" prompt</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <h4>Prompt Library Mistakes</h4>
      <ul style="font-size:.85rem;color:var(--dim);line-height:1.8;padding-left:1rem">
        <li>Saving prompts you've never actually tested</li>
        <li>Making the library too complex to maintain</li>
        <li>Not including the context that made the prompt work</li>
        <li>Hoarding 200 prompts instead of mastering 20</li>
        <li>Not updating prompts as your style evolves</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>Your homework:</strong> Create a prompt library document right now. Add your style anchor and 3 prompts that worked well for you this week. That's it. Three prompts. You'll build from there naturally -- if the foundation exists.</p>
  </div>
</div>
