<!-- SECTION 1: THE TALENT REALITY -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Reality</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">You're not competing with Google for talent. You don't need to.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">The AI talent war narrative is overblown for most organizations. Yes, top ML researchers command $500K+ salaries and go to frontier labs. But implementing AI in your business doesn't require frontier research. It requires <strong>people who understand your business problems and know how to apply existing AI tools to solve them</strong>.</p>
  <p class="section-text">That's a fundamentally different hiring profile. And it's one you can actually compete for.</p>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 2: ROLES THAT MATTER -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Roles</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Six AI roles, ranked by when you need them.</h2>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>1. AI Champion (Hire first)</strong>
      <p><em>$120-180K | Internal promotion or external hire</em></p>
      <p>A senior leader who owns the AI strategy. Not necessarily technical. Must understand business operations, change management, and vendor evaluation. Often your best ops or strategy person with AI curiosity.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>2. AI/ML Engineer</strong>
      <p><em>$150-250K | External hire</em></p>
      <p>Builds and maintains AI integrations. Connects APIs, fine-tunes models, builds data pipelines. You need this role when you move from buying AI tools to building custom AI workflows.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>3. Data Engineer</strong>
      <p><em>$130-200K | External hire</em></p>
      <p>Gets your data AI-ready. Cleans, structures, and pipelines data from across your organization. Without this role, your AI tools starve. Garbage in, garbage out remains the #1 AI failure mode.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>4. Prompt Engineer / AI Analyst</strong>
      <p><em>$80-140K | Internal upskill or external hire</em></p>
      <p>Designs and optimizes AI prompts, workflows, and use cases for business teams. This is often your most tech-savvy analyst or operations person who learns prompt engineering and AI tool configuration.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>5. AI Ethics / Governance Lead</strong>
      <p><em>$130-180K | External hire (often fractional)</em></p>
      <p>Ensures AI use complies with regulations, company values, and risk tolerance. Critical in healthcare, finance, and any regulated industry. Can start as a fractional or consulting role.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>6. Change Management Lead</strong>
      <p><em>$110-160K | Internal promotion</em></p>
      <p>Manages the human side of AI adoption. Training, communication, resistance management. Frequently overlooked. The organizations that skip this role have 3x higher AI project failure rates.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <strong>The minimum viable AI team:</strong> An AI Champion + a Prompt Engineer + a fractional Data Engineer. Total cost: $250-350K/year. This gets you started with SaaS AI tools, basic custom workflows, and clean data. Scale from there based on results.
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 3: BUILD VS UPSKILL -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Decision</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Upskill vs. hire: the framework.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Internal upskilling is almost always the right first move. Your existing team already understands your business, your data, and your customers. That domain knowledge takes years to build and is more valuable than AI expertise, which can be taught in months.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Upskill when...</strong>
      <ul>
        <li>The role requires deep business domain knowledge</li>
        <li>You have curious, adaptable team members</li>
        <li>The AI skills needed are tool-based (prompting, no-code AI)</li>
        <li>Timeline allows 3-6 months of learning</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Hire when...</strong>
      <ul>
        <li>You need ML engineering or data engineering skills</li>
        <li>The role requires expertise your team can't acquire quickly</li>
        <li>You're building custom AI products or models</li>
        <li>Speed matters more than institutional knowledge</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>The upskilling investment that pays off fastest:</strong> Send 3-5 high-potential employees through structured AI training (like this course). Give them 20% time to experiment with AI tools on real business problems. Within 90 days, you'll know who your internal AI champions are. That's cheaper and faster than any recruiting process.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 4: TEAM STRUCTURES -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Structure</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Three team structures that work.</h2>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Centralized AI Center of Excellence</strong>
      <p><em>Best for: Large enterprises, regulated industries</em></p>
      <p>One dedicated AI team serves the entire organization. Sets standards, manages vendor relationships, ensures governance. Pros: consistency, control. Cons: can become a bottleneck, may not understand each department's needs deeply.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Hub-and-Spoke</strong>
      <p><em>Best for: Mid-market, multi-department organizations</em></p>
      <p>Small central AI team (the hub) sets strategy and standards. Embedded AI specialists in each department (the spokes) implement use cases. Pros: balances governance with speed. Cons: requires coordination overhead.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Distributed / Embedded</strong>
      <p><em>Best for: Small-mid organizations, tech-forward cultures</em></p>
      <p>No dedicated AI team. Instead, every department has AI-trained members who implement their own solutions within company guidelines. Pros: fast, autonomous. Cons: inconsistency, harder to govern, requires strong AI-literate culture.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="tip-box">
    <strong>Start with Hub-and-Spoke.</strong> It gives you the control of centralized with the speed of distributed. Promote your AI Champion to lead the hub. Identify one AI-curious person in each major department as your spokes. Formalize with budget and goals within 90 days.
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 5: BUDGET TEMPLATE -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">The Budget</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">A realistic AI budget template.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Most AI budgets fail because they account for tools but forget training, change management, and data preparation. Here's a complete picture.</p>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>Year 1 AI Budget Framework (Mid-Market)</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>People (60%)</strong> — AI Champion salary, upskilling programs, fractional hires. Budget: $200-400K</li>
      <li><strong>Tools & Platform (20%)</strong> — SaaS AI tools, API costs, cloud compute. Budget: $60-150K</li>
      <li><strong>Data Preparation (10%)</strong> — Data cleaning, integration, pipeline development. Budget: $30-75K</li>
      <li><strong>Training & Change Management (10%)</strong> — Team training, workshops, communication, documentation. Budget: $30-75K</li>
    </ul>
    <p><em>Total Year 1 range: $320K - $700K depending on organization size and ambition.</em></p>
  </div>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Year 1 goal</strong>
      <p>Prove ROI on 2-3 use cases. Build internal capability. Establish governance. The budget should pay for itself through efficiency gains by Q3-Q4.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Year 2 adjustment</strong>
      <p>Double down on what worked. People costs shift from hiring to retention. Tool costs may increase as you expand. Data costs decrease as infrastructure matures.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>The CFO pitch</strong>
      <p>Frame AI budget as investment with measurable ROI, not as a cost center. Track hours saved, revenue influenced, and cost reduction from Day 1. Report monthly to the executive team.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>Common budget mistake</strong>
      <p>Spending 80% on tools and 20% on people. Invert that ratio. The best AI tools in the world are worthless without people who know how to deploy them effectively.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<!-- SECTION 6: LAB EXERCISE -->
<div class="lesson-section">
  <span class="section-label">Lab Exercise</span>
  <h2 class="section-title">Draft your AI team plan.</h2>
  <p class="section-text">Take 15 minutes right now to answer these four questions. Write the answers down. This becomes page one of your AI strategy.</p>

  <div class="prompt-grid">
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>1. Who is your AI Champion?</strong>
      <p>Name a specific person (or role to hire) who will own AI strategy in your organization. What makes them right for this?</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>2. What's your team structure?</strong>
      <p>Centralized, Hub-and-Spoke, or Distributed? Why? Map out the first three roles you'll fill.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>3. What's your Year 1 budget?</strong>
      <p>Use the 60/20/10/10 framework. Put real numbers to each category for your organization's size.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="prompt-card">
      <strong>4. What's your 90-day milestone?</strong>
      <p>What will be true about your AI team 90 days from now? Be specific. "AI Champion hired and two use cases in pilot" is better than "explore AI opportunities."</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="callout">
    <p><strong>Take this plan to your next leadership meeting.</strong> It doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be specific enough to generate a real conversation about priorities, resources, and timeline. The organizations that win with AI are the ones that commit early and iterate fast.</p>
  </div>
</div>
