## narration
Here is why this matters to you personally. AI already decides things about your life. It scores your loan application. It filters your resume before a human sees it. It decides what news you see, what ads you get, what prices you are offered online. If that AI is biased, opaque, or wrong — you pay the price. And most people never even know it happened.
highlight: loan application, resume, news, biased, opaque, you pay the price

## narration
AI ethics breaks down into five core areas. Bias — making sure AI does not discriminate based on race, gender, age, or other protected characteristics. Privacy — controlling how your data is collected, stored, and used. Transparency — knowing when AI is making decisions about you. Accountability — having someone responsible when AI causes harm. And safety — preventing AI from being used in dangerous ways.
highlight: bias, privacy, transparency, accountability, safety

## narration
The good news is that you do not need a philosophy degree to engage with AI ethics. As a user, you have power. You can ask companies whether they tested their AI for bias. You can choose tools that are transparent about how they work. You can push back when AI is used to make decisions without human oversight. Ethical AI starts with informed users.
highlight: you have power, ask companies, choose tools, push back, informed users

## narration
And if you build with AI or use it at work, the stakes are even higher. Deploying biased AI can expose your company to lawsuits. Using AI without transparency erodes customer trust. Collecting data without consent violates regulations in most countries. AI ethics is not just morally right — it is a business imperative.
highlight: lawsuits, customer trust, regulations, business imperative
